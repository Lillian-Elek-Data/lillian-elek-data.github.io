Problem Statement

Stroke is a leading cause of death and disability worldwide, but many risk factors are preventable with early intervention. This project aimed to build a predictive model that could identify high-risk patients based on readily available clinical and demographic data.

Business Impact: Early identification of high-risk patients enables healthcare providers to implement preventive measures, potentially saving lives and reducing healthcare costs associated with stroke treatment and rehabilitation.

Challenges

  • Class Imbalance: Only 4.9% of patients experienced strokes, requiring careful handling of imbalanced data
  • Missing Data: Real-world healthcare data contained missing values that needed strategic imputation
  • Feature Selection: Identifying which clinical markers were truly predictive versus correlated
  • Model Interpretability: Ensuring the model could be explained to healthcare professionals for clinical adoption

Methodology

This project followed a rigorous data science workflow, emphasizing reproducibility and statistical validity.

  • 1. Data Acquisition & Exploration

    Analyzed 5,109 patient records with 11 features including age, BMI, glucose levels, and medical history. Conducted exploratory data analysis to understand distributions, correlations, and data quality issues.

  • 2. Data Cleaning & Preprocessing

    Handled missing values using domain-informed imputation strategies. Encoded categorical variables (work type, residence, smoking status) and scaled numerical features for model compatibility.

  • 3. Feature Engineering

    Applied polynomial and log features for non-linear relationships between predictors and stroke risk.

  • 4. Handling Class Imbalance

    Implemented class weights = 'balanced' to handle imbalance of stroke patients to find optimal model.

  • 5. Model Development & Training

    Built logistic regression model using 80/20 train-test split with stratification to preserve class distribution.

  • 6. Statistical Diagnostics

    Performed hypothesis testing on coefficient significance. Checked for multicollinearity using VIF (Variance Inflation Factor). Validated model assumptions including linearity of log-odds.

  • 7. Model Evaluation & Validation

    Evaluated using accuracy, precision, recall, F1-score, and ROC-AUC. Analyzed confusion matrix to understand false positive/negative trade-offs. Examined feature coefficients for interpretability and clinical relevance.

Key Findings & Insights

Top Predictive Features

The model identified several strong predictors of stroke risk:

  • Age: Most significant predictorâ€”each standard deviation increase associated with a 5.9x increase in stroke odds.
  • Hypertension: Patients with hypertension nearly doubles stroke odds
  • Average Glucose Level: Elevated glucose shows a smaller but positive association

Model Performance Analysis

Precision vs. Recall Trade-off: The model achieved 73% overall accuracy with a deliberate bias toward sensitivity. Breaking down the stroke prediction performance:

  • Recall (80%): The model successfully identifies 4 out of 5 patients who will actually experience a strokeâ€”critical for early intervention
  • Precision (13%): When the model flags a patient as high-risk, it's correct 13% of the time, resulting in false alarms for preventive screening
  • Clinical Context: In healthcare screening, this trade-off prioritizes catching actual stroke cases over minimizing false positives. Missing a real stroke (false negative) has far more serious consequences than flagging a low-risk patient for additional screening (false positive)
  • No Stroke Class (99% precision, 72% recall): The model is highly reliable at identifying low-risk patients, correctly clearing 99% of patients it flags as low-risk

Addressing the Class Imbalance Challenge

With only 4.9% of patients experiencing strokes in the dataset (50 stroke cases vs. 972 no-stroke cases), this is a highly imbalanced classification problem. The model's performance reflects a strategic decision:

  • Optimized for Recall: Used class weighting to ensure the minority class (stroke) isn't overlooked
  • Acceptable False Positives: In preventive medicine, it's medically and ethically preferable to over-screen than to miss high-risk patients
  • Next Steps for Improvement: Additional feature enhancement with additional clinical variables, tiered decision framework, or model comparison to capture best fitting model.

Business Applications

  • Screening Tool: Deploy as a first-pass screening system to identify patients who need comprehensive stroke risk assessment
  • Resource Allocation: Direct preventive care resources (lifestyle counseling, medication management) to flagged patients
  • Risk Stratification: Segment patient populations by risk level to prioritize follow-up care and monitoring frequency
  • Early Intervention Pipeline: Integrate with clinical workflows to trigger automated alerts for high-risk patients, enabling proactive intervention
  • Cost-Benefit Analysis: While false positives generate screening costs, catching actual strokes early prevents the far greater costs of emergency treatment and long-term disability care
