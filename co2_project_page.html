Emissions by the Equation: Predicting CO₂ with OLS

A regression-powered deep dive into what drives state-level carbon emissions across the United States.

This project uses Ordinary Least Squares regression to predict CO₂ emissions based on environmental and economic factors. Through log transformations, feature selection, and comprehensive diagnostics, I built a high-accuracy model revealing the key drivers of carbon emissions at the state level.

Problem Statement

Climate change represents one of the most pressing challenges of our time, with carbon dioxide emissions being a primary driver of global warming. Understanding what factors contribute most significantly to CO₂ emissions at the state level is essential for developing effective environmental policies and reduction strategies.

However, emissions patterns are influenced by a complex interplay of environmental, economic, and demographic factors. Without a quantitative understanding of these relationships, policymakers struggle to prioritize interventions and allocate resources effectively.

The Challenge: Build a predictive model that can accurately estimate state-level CO₂ emissions based on measurable characteristics, identify the most significant emission drivers, and provide actionable insights for emission reduction strategies.

National Impact

Environmental Policy & Planning

This analysis provides critical insights for state and federal environmental agencies working to meet emissions reduction targets. By quantifying which factors most significantly impact CO₂ output, policymakers can:

  • Prioritize interventions by focusing on the highest-impact emission drivers
  • Forecast future emissions under different policy scenarios and economic conditions
  • Benchmark state performance and identify states that outperform or underperform expectations
  • Allocate resources efficiently to maximize emission reductions per dollar invested
  • Set realistic targets based on each state's unique characteristics and constraints

With states contributing differently to national emissions based on their economic structure, population density, and energy mix, understanding these drivers enables more nuanced, effective climate policy that accounts for regional differences while working toward collective national goals.

Challenges

  • Skewed Distributions:Many environmental and economic variables exhibited significant positive skew, violating OLS assumptions. Required careful transformation strategy to achieve normality without losing interpretability.

  • Multicollinearity: Economic and population variables showed high intercorrelation, creating multicollinearity issues. Required careful feature selection to identify independent predictors.

  • Scale Differences: Variables measured on vastly different scales (e.g., population vs. emissions) complicated direct comparison. Log transformations helped normalize scale differences.

  • Assumption Validation: Meeting the four remaining OLS assumptions (linearity, independence, normality, homoscedasticity) simultaneously required iterative model refinement and diagnostic testing.

Methodology

This project follows a rigorous statistical modeling workflow with emphasis on assumption validation and interpretability.

  • 1. Data Preparation & Exploration & Preprocessing

    Loaded 2009 state-level environmental and economic data. Conducted exploratory data analysis to understand distributions, identify outliers, and examine relationships between variables. Checked for missing values and data quality issues.

  • 2. Data Preprocessing & Train/Test Split

    Applied log transformations to handle right-skewed distributions and capture multiplicative relationships. The dataset was then split into training and testing sets 80/20 split. During the split I preformed numerical scaling for model compatibility.

  • 3. Model Development (Training data)

    Implemented OLS regression using statsmodels. Conducted a backwards and forwards stepwise regression to identify the optimal predictor combination that maximizes explanatory power while maintaining statistical validity.

  • 4. OLS Model Assumption Validation

    To ensure the statistical validity and interpretability of the OLS model, I conducted comprehensive residual analysis including Q-Q plots, residual vs. fitted plots, and scale-location plots. Validated linearity, normality, homoscedasticity, and independence assumptions. These checks improve confidence in the model’s conclusions.

  • 5. Model Evaluation

    Using scikit-learn, the finalized feature set was used to test the regression model. Performance was assessed using R², RMSE, and MAE to capture the model fit and predictive performance.

Key Findings & Insights

Top Predictive Features

The analysis revealed several key drivers of state-level CO₂ emissions:

Economic Activity

State GDP and industrial output showed strong positive correlation with emissions, highlighting the carbon intensity of economic production.

Energy Consumption

Total energy use emerged as a primary predictor, with different energy sources (fossil fuels vs. renewables) showing distinct impact patterns.

Population Density

Population size and density influenced emissions, though the relationship was moderated by urbanization patterns and transportation infrastructure.

Log-Transformed Effects

Logarithmic transformations revealed multiplicative relationships, showing that percentage changes in predictors correspond to percentage changes in emissions.

Model Performance Analysis

Training Performance

  • High R²: Model explained a substantial proportion of variance in state-level CO₂ emissions
  • Adjusted R²: Remained high after adjusting for number of predictors, indicating genuine predictive power
  • F-statistic significance: Overall model showed strong statistical significance (p < 0.001)

Testing Performance

  • Low RMSE: Predictions on unseen test data showed minimal error
  • Consistent accuracy: Model maintained performance across train and test sets, indicating no overfitting
  • Residual patterns: Residuals showed random scatter around zero with no systematic patterns

Assumption Validation

  • Linearity: Residual plots confirmed linear relationships between predictors and outcome
  • Normality: Q-Q plots showed residuals follow normal distribution
  • Homoscedasticity: Scale-location plots demonstrated constant variance of residuals
  • Independence: No autocorrelation detected in residuals (Durbin-Watson ≈ 2.0)

Next Steps for Improvement

While this model demonstrates strong performance, several enhancements could further improve accuracy and insights:

  • Time-series extension: Incorporate data from multiple years to capture temporal trends and policy impacts
  • Advanced algorithms: Compare OLS performance against Ridge, Lasso, and Random Forest regression models
  • Interaction effects: Explore how combinations of variables (e.g., population × energy mix) jointly influence emissions
  • Sector-specific analysis: Break down emissions by sector (transportation, industrial, residential) for more targeted insights
  • Cross-validation: Implement k-fold cross-validation for more robust performance estimates
  • Feature importance: Calculate standardized coefficients and partial dependence plots to better communicate relative importance
  • Outlier analysis: Investigate states with high residuals to understand unique emission patterns
